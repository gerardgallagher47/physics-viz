Venturi Effect — fast throat, low pressure

Continuity and Bernoulli together: pinch the pipe, the flow accelerates, and the static pressure drops.

Why a pipe's narrow throat has the lowest pressure

The same volume of fluid must pass every cross-section each second, so where the pipe narrows the flow has to speed up. Bernoulli then says faster flow carries lower pressure — so the throat is at once the fastest and the lowest-pressure point.

Go deeper

The throat area is one-third of the inlet, so continuity forces v₂ = 3v₁. Substituting into Bernoulli gives the pressure drop ΔP = ½ρ(v₂² − v₁²), which grows with the square of the inlet speed.

This is exactly how a carburettor, a perfume atomiser and a Venturi flow-meter work — the low-pressure throat draws in or measures fluid.
