When does a block stay put on a slope?

A block on an incline feels three forces: its weight pulling straight down, the surface pushing back (the normal force), and friction gripping along the slope. It stays still only while friction can cover the share of gravity that drags it down-slope.

No-slip condition
How it maps to the diagram
Red arrow — weight W = mg, straight down
Blue arrow — normal force N = mg cos θ
Amber arrow — friction f, pointing up the slope
Purple arrow — net force (appears only when it slides)
Dashed red — weight split into mg sin θ (down-slope) and mg cos θ (into surface)
Go deeper

Resolve the forces along and perpendicular to the slope. The perpendicular balance gives N = mg cos θ. Along the slope, gravity pulls with mg sin θ while friction resists up to a maximum of μₛN.

Set mg sin θ = μₛ mg cos θ and the mass cancels: the block slips the instant tan θ > μₛ — a heavier block does not slide sooner. Assumes a rigid block and dry (Coulomb) friction.

Parameters

m — mass2.0 kg
θ — slope angle15°
μₛ — static friction0.30
g — gravity9.81 m/s²
